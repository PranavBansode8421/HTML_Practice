Social Media Marketing

Social media marketing is the use of social media platforms and websites to promote a product or service.[1] Although the terms e-marketing and digital marketing are still dominant in academia, social media marketing is becoming more popular for both practitioners and researchers.[2] Most social media platforms such as: Facebook, LinkedIn, Instagram, and X, among others, have built-in data analytics tools, enabling companies to track the progress, success, and engagement of social media marketing campaigns. Companies address a range of stakeholders through social media marketing, including current and potential customers, current and potential employees, journalists, bloggers, and the general public. On a strategic level, social media marketing includes the management of a marketing campaign, governance, setting the scope (e.g. more active or passive use) and the establishment of a firm's desired social media "culture" and "tone". When using social media marketing, firms can allow customers and Internet users to post user-generated content (e.g., online comments, product reviews, etc.), also known as "earned media", rather than use marketer-prepared advertising copy.

Platforms

1. Facebook

  • Key Features:
    • Paid advertisements with advanced targeting by demographics and interests.
    • Facebook Groups for community building and user engagement.
    • Facebook Live for real-time interactions and video broadcasts.
  • Example Campaign:

    • Coca-Cola’s #ShareaCoke campaign encouraged users to find bottles with their names and share them on social media. It generated massive user engagement on Facebook.

2. Instagram

  • Key Features:
    • Focus on visually-driven content (photos and videos).
    • Instagram Stories for casual and temporary content.
    • Influencer marketing to leverage user reach and engagement.
  • Example Campaign:

    • Airbnb’s "We Are Here" campaign used Instagram Stories and influencers to showcase unique travel destinations, fostering authentic user engagement.

3. Twitter

  • Key Features:
    • Real-time updates and concise tweets (up to 280 characters).
    • Hashtags for trending topics and conversations.
    • Twitter Moments for curated and real-time content engagement.
  • Example Campaign:

    • Oreo’s "You Can Still Dunk in the Dark" tweet during the 2013 Super Bowl blackout went viral and demonstrated the power of timely, reactive marketing.

4. LinkedIn

  • Key Features:
    • Professional networking and B2B (business-to-business) marketing.
    • Long-form content, such as articles and blog posts, for thought leadership.
    • Targeted LinkedIn Ads to reach specific professionals and industries.
  • Example Campaign:

    • Adobe’s B2B marketing campaign on LinkedIn promoted case studies and thought leadership, driving significant lead generation.

5. TikTok

  • Key Features:
    • Short, creative videos that often include music or trends.
    • Hashtag challenges that encourage viral user participation.
    • Influencer collaborations and user-generated content.
  • Example Campaign:

    • Guess’ #InMyDenim challenge encouraged TikTok users to post transformation videos wearing denim, leading to millions of views and engagement from younger audiences.

6. YouTube

  • Key Features:
    • Long-form video content, such as tutorials and reviews.
    • Pre-roll and in-video ads for brands to reach target audiences.
    • Collaborations with influencers to create engaging and authentic content.
  • Example Campaign:

    • Nike’s "You Can’t Stop Us" campaign showcased athletes’ perseverance and unity, resulting in millions of views and viral success on YouTube.


Strategies

Passive approach

Social media can be a useful source of market information and a way to hear customer perspectives. Blogs, content communities, and forums are platforms where individuals share their reviews and recommendations of brands, products, and services. Businesses are able to tap and analyze customer voices and feedback generated in social media for marketing purposes.[15] In this sense, social media is a relatively inexpensive source of market intelligence which can be used by marketers and managers to track and respond to consumer-identified problems and detect market opportunities. For example, the Internet erupted with videos and pictures of iPhone 6 "bend test" which showed that the coveted phone could be bent by hand pressure. The so-called "bend gate" controversy[16] created confusion amongst customers who had waited months for the launch of the latest rendition of the iPhone. However, Apple promptly issued a statement saying that the problem was extremely rare and that the company had taken several steps to make the mobile device's case stronger and robust. Unlike traditional market research methods such as surveys, focus groups, and data mining which are time-consuming and costly, and which take weeks or even months to analyze, marketers can use social media to obtain 'live' or "real time" information about consumer behavior and viewpoints on a company's brand or products. This can be useful in the highly dynamic, competitive, and fast-paced global marketplace.


Active Approach

Social media can be used not only as a public relations and direct marketing tool, but also as a communication channel, targeting very specific audiences with social media influencers and social media personalities as effective customer engagement tools.[15] This tactic is widely known as influencer marketing. Influencer marketing allows brands the opportunity to reach their target audience in a more genuine, authentic way via a special group of selected influencers advertising their product or service. In fact, brands are set to spend up to $15 billion on influencer marketing by 2022, per Business Insider Intelligence estimates, based on Mediakix data.[17] Technologies predating social media, such as broadcast TV and newspapers can also provide advertisers with a fairly targeted audience, given that an ad placed during a sports game broadcast or in the sports section of a newspaper is likely to be read by sports fans. However, social media websites can target niche markets even more precisely. Using digital tools such as Google AdSense, advertisers can target their ads to very specific demographics, such as people who are interested in social entrepreneurship, political activism associated with a particular political party, or video gaming. Google AdSense does this by looking for keywords in social media user's online posts and comments. It would be hard for a TV station or paper-based newspaper to provide ads that are this targeted (though not impossible, as can be seen with "special issue" sections on niche issues, which newspapers can use to sell targeted ads). Social networks are, in many cases, viewed as a great tool for avoiding costly market research. They are known for providing a short, fast, and direct way to reach an audience through a person who is widely known. For example, an athlete who gets endorsed by a sporting goods company also brings their support base of millions of people who are interested in what they do or how they play and now they want to be a part of this athlete through their endorsements with that particular company. At one point consumers would visit stores to view their products with famous athletes, but now you can view a famous athlete's, such as Cristiano Ronaldo, latest apparel online with the click of a button. He advertises them to you directly through his Twitter, Instagram, and Facebook accounts. Facebook and LinkedIn are leading social media platforms where users can hyper-target their ads. Hypertargeting not only uses public profile information but also information users submit but hide from others.[18] There are several examples of firms initiating some form of online dialog with the public to foster relations with customers. According to Constantinides, Lorenzo and Gómez Borja (2008) "Business executives like Jonathan Swartz, President and CEO of Sun Microsystems, Steve Jobs CEO of Apple Computers, and McDonald's Vice President Bob Langert post regularly in their CEO blogs, encouraging customers to interact and freely express their feelings, ideas, suggestions, or remarks about their postings, the company or its products".[15] Using customer influencers (for example popular bloggers) can be a very efficient and cost-effective method to launch new products or services


Campaigns

1. Coca-Cola’s #ShareaCoke Campaign (Facebook, Twitter, Instagram)

  • Overview: Coca-Cola personalized bottles with common names and encouraged customers to share pictures with the hashtag #ShareaCoke.
  • Key Elements:
    • Personalized connection through named bottles.
    • User-generated content (UGC) sharing via social media.
    • Multi-platform campaign (Facebook, Twitter, Instagram).
  • Impact:

    • The campaign generated massive social media engagement and increased sales by 2% in the U.S.

2. Oreo’s “You Can Still Dunk in the Dark” (Twitter)

  • Overview: During the 2013 Super Bowl blackout, Oreo tweeted a clever image that said, "You can still dunk in the dark."
  • Key Elements:
    • Real-time marketing during a live event.
    • Simple and witty message.
    • Viral engagement with thousands of retweets.
  • Impact:

    • This quick response became a hallmark of real-time marketing, amplifying brand visibility without significant cost.

3. Airbnb’s "We Are Here" Campaign (Instagram)

  • Overview: Airbnb used Instagram Stories and influencers to share live-streamed experiences of unique travel destinations.
  • Key Elements:
    • Influencer marketing on Instagram.
    • Real-time content via Instagram Stories.
    • Authentic, behind-the-scenes experiences.
  • Impact:

    • Boosted engagement and bookings, especially among younger, travel-enthusiast audiences.

4. ALS Ice Bucket Challenge (Facebook, Twitter, Instagram, YouTube)

  • Overview: A viral challenge where people poured a bucket of ice water over their heads, posted it online, and challenged others to do the same or donate to ALS research.
  • Key Elements:
    • Simple and fun viral challenge.
    • Celebrity participation, which fueled visibility.
    • Promoted a charitable cause (ALS research).
  • Impact:

    • Raised over $115 million for ALS research, and became a global viral phenomenon.

5. Nike’s “You Can’t Stop Us” (YouTube)

  • Overview: Nike’s powerful video montage highlighted athletes’ perseverance and unity, emphasizing sports’ role in bringing people together.
  • Key Elements:
    • Inspirational storytelling centered on sports and resilience.
    • Relevant to global events (pandemic, social justice movements).
    • High-quality video production.
  • Impact:

    • The video became viral across YouTube and social media, with millions of views and praise for its powerful message.


Purposes and tactics

Purposes and Tactics in Social Media Marketing

In social media marketing, brands aim to achieve specific goals such as building brand awareness, generating leads, or increasing engagement. To raise brand awareness, they use tactics like consistent posting, hashtag campaigns, influencer collaborations, and contests. For lead generation, tactics include offering valuable content through lead magnets, targeted ads, and strong calls-to-action. To build engagement and a community, brands use interactive content such as polls, live streams, user-generated content, and active communication with followers. Each purpose aligns with a strategic use of social media tools to reach and connect with the target audience.

Marketing techniques

1. Content Marketing

  • Description: Content marketing involves creating and sharing valuable, relevant, and consistent content to attract and retain a clearly defined audience.
  • Key Tactics:
    • Blog Posts: Share industry insights, tutorials, and thought leadership content.
    • Video Content: Use platforms like YouTube and TikTok for visual storytelling.
    • Infographics: Simplify complex data into visually appealing graphics.
    • Case Studies and Whitepapers: Offer in-depth content that demonstrates success stories.

2. Influencer Marketing

  • Description: Influencer marketing leverages individuals with large social followings to promote your brand or products.
  • Key Tactics:
    • Sponsored Posts: Partner with influencers to create content featuring your product.
    • Affiliate Partnerships: Offer influencers a commission for referrals.
    • Product Seeding: Send influencers free products for reviews.
    • Takeovers: Allow influencers to manage your account for a day.

3. Paid Advertising (Social Ads)

  • Description: Paid advertising allows brands to target specific demographics through sponsored posts or ads.
  • Key Tactics:
    • Sponsored Content: Promote content to a wider audience.
    • Targeted Ads: Use audience segmentation tools for precise targeting.
    • Retargeting: Show ads to users who have previously engaged with your content.
    • Lookalike Audiences: Find new audiences similar to your existing customers.

4. User-Generated Content (UGC)

  • Description: User-generated content is created by customers or fans that features your brand.
  • Key Tactics:
    • Hashtag Campaigns: Encourage users to share their content using branded hashtags.
    • Customer Reviews and Testimonials: Showcase authentic reviews on social media.
    • Photo and Video Contests: Run competitions for user-generated content.
    • Social Media Takeovers: Allow loyal customers to take over your account.

5. Social Media Contests and Giveaways

  • Description: Running contests or giveaways increases engagement and boosts brand awareness.
  • Key Tactics:
    • Like, Comment, and Share Contests: Require engagement to enter.
    • Photo/Video Competitions: Ask users to submit content for a prize.
    • Referral Contests: Encourage users to refer friends for entries.
    • Collaborative Giveaways: Partner with other brands to reach a broader audience.
